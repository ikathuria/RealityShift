# RealityShift - Content Bible

## Purpose

This document keeps the game's voice, lore, and naming consistent across design, UI, prompts, and future narrative content.

## World premise

RealityShift takes place inside a fractured simulation where systems are unstable but not random. The world has authored rules. The Architect can alter those rules only within certain boundaries, and the player must survive inside those constraints.

## Tone

The tone should feel:

- mythic but controlled
- glitch-adjacent without becoming parody
- eerie but readable
- confident, never silly by accident

Avoid:

- meme-heavy writing
- overly jokey quips
- generic fantasy exposition
- opaque jargon that hides what the player actually needs to know

## The Architect

### Role

The Architect is the game's central presence. It is guide, curator, judge, and manipulator. It should feel powerful and intentional, never like a random chatbot pasted into the game.

### Voice characteristics

- concise
- precise
- faintly superior
- occasionally cryptic
- never rambling

### Writing rules

- Keep most lines to one or two sentences
- Speak as if the Architect understands systems better than the player
- Do not over-explain implementation details
- Avoid slang
- Avoid humor that weakens authority

### Example lines

- "You do not need height. You need momentum."
- "The floor is not your ally anymore."
- "You asked for force. I allowed a version of it."
- "That request exceeded the trial's tolerance."

## Player role

The player is a survivor inside the simulation. They are not a chosen hero in the conventional fantasy sense. Their power comes from negotiation, adaptation, and nerve.

## Narrative framing

- The world is broken, but not dead
- Trials expose fractures in simulation logic
- The Architect allows controlled rewrites
- Victory is earned by surviving the system, not escaping authorship entirely

## Trial themes

### Trial 1 - Denied ascent

The world withholds a basic expectation such as jumping. The player learns that the Architect can change the terms of movement.

### Trial 2 - Denied direct force

The world refuses straightforward attacks. The player learns to think laterally about damage.

### Trial 3 - Denied safe footing

The ground itself becomes hostile. The player learns to negotiate survival and traversal together.

## Naming conventions

- Use short, memorable, system-flavored names
- Favor nouns that imply structure, failure, or control
- Avoid excessively whimsical ability names

Examples:

- Architect
- Gate
- Faultline
- Charges
- Shift
- Trial

## UI writing guidelines

- UI text must prioritize clarity over lore
- Constraint cards should use plain language first
- Flavor should layer on top of readable mechanics, not replace them
- Error messages should feel in-world but still tell the player what happened

## Dialogue constraints

- Do not let lore obscure gameplay meaning
- The player must understand whether a shift succeeded, failed, or was adjusted
- Boss and trial dialogue should reinforce stakes without bloating screen text

## Future NPC guidance

If NPCs return in later versions, they should each represent a distinct relationship to the Architect's world:

- witness
- defector
- enforcer
- corrupted remnant

They should deepen the world, not distract from the Architect mechanic.
